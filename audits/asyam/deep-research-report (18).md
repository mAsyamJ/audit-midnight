# Morpho Midnight Security Research Assessment

## Bottom line

Morpho Midnight is a fixed-maturity, isolated-market lending protocol where liquidity is quoted through offchain offers and only sourced at execution time. That architecture matters for bug hunting: the core accounting path is concentrated in `take`, `repay`, `withdraw`, `withdrawCollateral`, and `liquidate`, while residual risk clusters around callbacks, ratifiers, gates, and the periphery bundler rather than around simple pool math. The protocol authors also published a broad Certora suite covering core accounting invariants, liquidation bounds, authorization, consumption, solvency, and fixed-point math, which materially lowers the probability of finding a vanilla arithmetic or “obvious invariant break” in the main happy path. citeturn19view0turn17view0turn18view0

The strongest practical takeaway is this: if you want a winning submission, do **not** spend most of your time on generic ERC-20 return-value handling, simple balance accounting, or straightforward `take`/`liquidate` rounding complaints. Those areas are either directly documented, formally verified, or both. The highest-value remaining edges are the places where the protocol itself says it relies on assumptions or where the verification setup explicitly simplifies reality, especially callback reentrancy, delegated-operator abuse, bundled multi-market execution, and oracle-boundary behavior. citeturn13view0turn18view0turn45view3

## What the protocol already defends well

The repository’s public verification summary says the Certora specs cover: total-units accounting, “credit XOR debt” style position invariants, continuous-fee bounds, `take` and `liquidate` input-output consistency, monotonicity of `withdrawable` and claimable settlement fees, market creation well-formedness, loss-factor arithmetic, health preservation, liquidation profitability and liquidation-profit caps, `consumed` monotonicity, ratifier authorization, authorization controls, token solvency, explicit-payer guarantees, bitmap correctness, and fixed-point arithmetic properties. In other words, a large share of the classic “medium by direct invariant break” landscape has already been targeted. citeturn17view0turn18view0

The same summary also states the verification setup removed `multicall` by induction and, critically, assumed that external calls do **not** re-enter Midnight unless the property being checked is specifically about callbacks. That is the single most important research clue in the whole repo: the residual attack surface is not “can I make `mulDiv` overflow,” it is “can I compose callback-time state with another entry point in a way the model did not fully constrain.” citeturn18view0

The NatSpec in `Midnight.sol` reinforces that reading. It explicitly warns that Midnight expects tokens not to re-enter and to transfer exact requested amounts, that liquidations are locked only for the seller during `take` callbacks, that broad operator authorization is intentionally powerful, and that some behaviors with offers, fees, and hard forks are known design tradeoffs rather than hidden surprises. That means many superficially interesting ideas will get judged as documented assumptions or intended trust model rather than bugs. citeturn13view0

## Draft submission candidate around periphery market-mixing

The cleanest **code-level** candidate I would personally test first is in `MidnightBundles.sol`: the bundled entry points repeatedly state in comments that they “should only be called with the same market for all takes,” and that collateral transfers always use the **first** offer’s market. The code snippets available publicly line up with that warning: after filling takes, the bundle logic reads `Market memory market = takes[0].offer.market;` and then performs collateral withdrawals or collateral supplies against that market, without any visible equality check that all other `takes[i].offer.market` match `takes[0].offer.market`. citeturn44view0turn44view1turn44view3turn44view4

**Why this matters.** If the periphery is in scope, this is an unsafe execution surface rather than just a cosmetic input-validation issue. A heterogeneous bundle can produce loan-side execution in one market while collateral-side effects are applied to another. Even if the first market is only a tiny “anchor” fill and the real economic fill happens elsewhere, the post-fill collateral movement still targets `takes[0].offer.market`. That is the classic shape of a user-fund-loss bug in routers and bundlers: the contract silently assumes a precondition that it does not itself enforce. citeturn44view0turn44view1turn44view3turn44view4

**Impact view.** The right severity here depends on scope and judge attitude. If judges treat the bundler as production periphery that must fail safely on malformed input, this can be framed as a **Medium** because wrong-market execution can move collateral from or into an unintended market and can therefore cause direct user fund loss. If judges instead treat it as a convenience wrapper used only by the caller on their own behalf, they may push it to **Low** or even “known assumption,” because the caller is the one constructing the malformed bundle. citeturn44view1turn44view3turn44view4

**Likelihood view.** I would rate likelihood as non-trivial. The code comments themselves acknowledge the precondition, and comments exist because the implementation does not appear to hard-enforce it in the shown paths. This is exactly the type of issue that slips through when the core protocol is strongly verified but the periphery remains “best-effort” and less formally constrained. citeturn44view1turn18view0

**No-fork PoC sketch.** Build two markets with the same loan token but different collateral sets. Give the taker collateral only in Market A. Prepare a bundle where `takes[0]` is a minimal valid fill on Market A, `takes[1]` is the economically meaningful fill on Market B, and `collateralWithdrawals` points at Market A’s collateral index. Call `buyWithUnitsTargetAndWithdrawCollateral` or `buyWithAssetsTargetAndWithdrawCollateral`. The invariant to assert is that the successful economic fill and the collateral movement are applied to different markets because the withdrawal path always references `takes[0].offer.market`. If this reproduces cleanly, you have a crisp Cantina submission with deterministic local mocks and no fork requirement. citeturn44view0turn44view3

**Recommendation.** Enforce `toId(takes[i].offer.market) == toId(takes[0].offer.market)` for every `i` before any stateful action, and fail closed. Do not rely on comments as the only guardrail. A second-best mitigation is to split these bundler functions by market and make the market an explicit top-level parameter rather than something inferred from the first take. citeturn44view1turn44view3turn44view4

## Draft submission candidate around zero-cost bad-debt realization

The highest-upside **core** candidate is the liquidation design choice that explicitly allows `liquidate` to be called with both `seizedAssets == 0` and `repaidUnits == 0` in order to realize bad debt with zero token transfer. The code comments say so directly, and the contract’s documented liquidation and slashing model says bad debt is socialized among lenders through the market loss factor. The whitepaper and NatSpec both make clear that liquidation and health depend on oracle prices. citeturn47view0turn13view0turn19view0

**Why this matters.** If a market’s oracle can be temporarily forced too low, even for a single transaction, then liquidating with `(0, 0)` can permanently crystallize “bad debt” and slash lenders without requiring the liquidator to repay any loan token or seize any collateral. The relevant mechanics are all there in the public code: `liquidate` starts with `badDebt = originalDebt`, iterates active collateral positions using oracle prices to compute debt capacity, and explicitly permits the zero-input path for bad-debt realization. Once loss is realized, the repo’s documentation says lender positions are socialized through the market loss factor and slashed on later interaction. citeturn47view0turn24view7turn25view0turn13view0turn19view0

**Impact view.** If the competition treats oracle manipulation or stale/weak oracle design as in scope, this is potentially **High** because the loss is irreversible for lenders and can be triggered by a third party at zero repayment cost. If, however, Cantina judges treat arbitrary oracle risk as an external trust assumption of permissionless markets, this candidate may be rejected as design-dependent rather than protocol-broken. That framing question is the difference between a strong submission and a waste of time. citeturn47view0turn19view0turn13view0

**Likelihood view.** Under strong oracles, low. Under weak, spot, manipulable, or admin-controlled oracles, much higher. Midnight markets are permissionlessly creatable and isolated, the whitepaper explicitly allows arbitrary collateral/oracle configurations, and the NatSpec documents oracle-dependent liveness and health behavior rather than imposing TWAP/staleness protections in the protocol layer. citeturn19view0turn13view0turn22view2

**No-fork PoC sketch.** Use a mock oracle. Create a market with real-looking collateral and loan tokens. Let a borrower open a position that is healthy at honest price. In the exploit step, have the oracle return a sharply lower price for one transaction, then call `liquidate(market, collateralIndex, 0, 0, borrower, false, receiver, address(0), "")`. Assert that the borrower’s bad debt is realized, the market loss factor increases, and no repayment transfer occurs. Then restore the oracle price and show that lenders are still slashed because the loss-factor event has already been committed. This is the exact kind of deterministic local PoC Cantina reviewers like. citeturn47view0turn24view7turn25view0turn18view0

**Recommendation.** If you can get this accepted as in-scope, the clean fix is not a tiny patch. The protocol would need either oracle hardening requirements at market creation, delayed bad-debt realization, or a requirement that some positive repayment accompany socialization. Even a minimal repayment floor would raise the exploit cost and make one-block oracle underpricing materially less attractive. citeturn47view0turn19view0

## Highest-probability hidden bug class even if you do not submit it yet

The area I would hunt hardest next is callback-time reentrancy and state-ordering, even if I would **not** submit it without a proof. The best evidence is in the code itself: in `take`, position fields, `totalUnits`, and settlement-fee claims are updated **before** callbacks and before final seller health enforcement; in `repay`, debt is decreased and `withdrawable` is increased **before** the optional callback and before `safeTransferFrom` pulls repayment funds. At the same time, the Certora model explicitly says external calls are assumed not to re-enter unless a property is specifically about callbacks. citeturn46view0turn47view0turn18view0

That combination does **not** automatically imply an exploit, but it is exactly where hidden bugs survive after strong formal verification. The two best concrete probes are these. First, test whether a repay callback can consume newly created `withdrawable` or interact with same-token cross-market balances before repayment is actually settled. Second, test whether nested `take` calls inside seller callbacks can exploit the temporary liquidation lock to build states that are impossible in a single non-reentrant execution, then restore health just enough to survive the final check. The protocol deliberately allows seller callbacks while the seller is liquidation-locked, and the final check is literally `liquidationLocked(id, seller) || isHealthy(...)`, which is very helpful for composability and therefore very attractive for weird-state testing. citeturn47view0turn13view0

My current honest view is that this is a **high-priority hunting direction**, not a confirmed finding. Atomicity may ultimately save the protocol from direct theft in many of these compositions. But because this area sits exactly outside the strongest public proof coverage, it is where a non-obvious Medium or High is most likely still hiding if one exists. citeturn18view0turn46view0turn47view0

## PoC playbook and Cantina-ready framing

For this repo, you should bias toward **no-fork** PoCs first. Midnight’s architecture is local-test friendly: markets are created permissionlessly, callbacks are contract-based, oracles are external interfaces, and the key entry points expose all the state transitions you need. The best first-pass harness is a mock ERC-20, a mock oracle with mutable price, a malicious callback contract, and one or two helper users representing a borrower, lender, and liquidator. Because the protocol itself documents strong assumptions on token behavior, using standard mocks plus one adversarial callback usually gives cleaner judge-facing results than trying to argue from exotic token semantics. citeturn19view0turn13view0turn45view3

For submission quality, structure every report as: **Summary**, **Finding Description**, **Impact Explanation**, **Likelihood Explanation**, **Proof of Concept**, and **Recommendation**. In the PoC, assert a crisp invariant break rather than just showing “weird behavior.” For the periphery candidate, the invariant is “bundle economic fills and collateral side effects must target the same market.” For the bad-debt candidate, the invariant is “lenders must not be permanently slashed for zero repayment cost under a transient oracle excursion.” For callback research, the invariant is “no state made externally visible before settlement may be reentered in a way that causes lasting value extraction.” citeturn44view1turn47view0turn18view0

A draft title that is reasonably strong for the bundler issue would be: **Unvalidated multi-market bundles apply collateral movements to the first market, causing wrong-market collateral loss**. A draft title for the liquidation issue would be: **Zero-input liquidation socializes oracle-induced bad debt at zero cost, permanently slashing lenders**. I would only submit the second one if your local test demonstrates permanent lender loss with a realistic in-scope oracle assumption; otherwise keep it as a research branch, not a reputation risk. citeturn44view1turn47view0turn19view0

## Open questions and limitations

I did **not** execute the repo locally in this session, so I am being deliberately conservative about what I call “confirmed.” The mixed-market bundler candidate is the most direct code-level issue visible from the published snippets. The zero-cost bad-debt candidate is the highest-upside core candidate, but its judge outcome will strongly depend on whether oracle-manipulation assumptions are considered fair game for permissionless Midnight markets. The callback/reentrancy section is a research priority, not a submission on its own yet. citeturn44view0turn47view0turn18view0

I also did not parse the draft Spearbit and Blackthorn PDFs, so I am not claiming novelty relative to those audits.
